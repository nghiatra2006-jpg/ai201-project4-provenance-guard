# Provenance Guard — Planning Document

## Problem Statement

Creative platforms face a growing challenge: verifying whether posted content was written by the person sharing it or generated by AI and passed off as human work. Provenance Guard is a backend classification service that accepts a piece of text, runs it through a two-signal detection pipeline, returns a confidence score and a plain-language transparency label, and provides an appeals workflow for contested classifications.

---

## Architecture

### Submission Flow

```
POST /submit
     │
     ▼
[Input Validation]
  text, creator_id
     │
     ├────────────────────────────────┐
     ▼                                ▼
[Signal 1: LLM Classifier]    [Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics]
  Groq llama-3.3-70b              sentence_length_variance
  → llm_score (0.0–1.0)           type_token_ratio
    (1.0 = confident AI)           punctuation_density
                                → stylo_score (0.0–1.0)
                                    (1.0 = AI-like uniformity)
     │                                │
     └───────────┬────────────────────┘
                 ▼
     [Confidence Scoring]
       combined = 0.55 * llm_score + 0.45 * stylo_score
       → confidence (0.0–1.0)
       → attribution label tier
                 │
                 ▼
     [Transparency Label Generator]
       confidence ≥ 0.75  → "Likely AI-Generated"
       0.45 ≤ conf < 0.75 → "Uncertain"
       confidence < 0.45  → "Likely Human-Written"
                 │
                 ▼
     [Audit Logger]
       writes structured JSON entry to log
                 │
                 ▼
     [Response]
       content_id, attribution, confidence,
       llm_score, stylo_score, label, status
```

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM-Based Classification (Groq)

**What it measures:** Semantic and stylistic coherence holistically — whether the text "sounds like" AI-generated writing based on vocabulary choices, sentence flow, structural uniformity, phrasing patterns, and topic handling.

**Why it differs between human and AI text:** Large language models tend to produce text with smooth transitions, balanced paragraph structures, hedged but authoritative language ("it is important to note that..."), and a lack of genuine personal quirks, tangents, or contradictions. Human writing is messier, more idiosyncratic, and carries a personal voice.

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0.
- `1.0` = classifier is highly confident the text is AI-generated
- `0.0` = classifier is highly confident the text is human-written
- Values near `0.5` reflect genuine uncertainty

**How it's obtained:** A structured prompt is sent to `llama-3.3-70b-versatile` via Groq API. The prompt asks the model to analyze the text and return a JSON object with a numeric `score` field and a `reasoning` field. The score is extracted and normalized.

**Blind spots:**
- A human imitating AI style (e.g., writing formally for a professional context) will score high
- AI text with intentional imperfections injected by the user will score lower
- Very short texts (<50 words) give the model insufficient signal
- The model may be biased toward flagging non-native English writing as AI-generated because it is often more grammatically formal

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics (Pure Python)

**What it measures:** Statistical properties of writing style — specifically three metrics that empirically differ between AI and human text:

1. **Sentence Length Variance (SLV):** The variance in the number of words per sentence. AI-generated text tends to have very uniform sentence lengths; human writing varies more.
   - High variance → more human-like → lower AI score
   - Low variance → AI-like uniformity → higher AI score
   - Normalized to 0–1 by capping at a max variance of 200

2. **Type-Token Ratio (TTR):** The ratio of unique word types to total tokens. AI text tends to repeat vocabulary patterns and use common words predictably; humans use a more idiosyncratic mix.
   - High TTR → diverse vocabulary → lower AI score
   - Low TTR → repetitive vocabulary → higher AI score

3. **Punctuation Density (PD):** Ratio of punctuation characters to total characters. Human writing uses more varied punctuation (dashes, ellipses, colons in informal ways); AI text tends toward clean, simple punctuation.
   - The metric is normalized and compared to a known AI baseline

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0.
- `1.0` = text has statistical properties strongly resembling AI output
- `0.0` = text has statistical properties strongly resembling human output

**Combination into stylo_score:**
```
slv_score  = 1.0 - normalize(variance)   # low variance → high AI score
ttr_score  = 1.0 - ttr                    # low diversity → high AI score
pd_score   = normalize(punctuation_density)  # adjusted baseline

stylo_score = 0.4 * slv_score + 0.4 * ttr_score + 0.2 * pd_score
```

**Blind spots:**
- Academic or technical human writing (long, careful sentences) will show low variance, inflating the AI score
- Short creative poems with intentional repetition will score as AI-like
- Non-English text or texts with unusual character sets will break tokenization assumptions
- A text of fewer than ~3 sentences cannot produce meaningful variance

## Confidence Scoring and Uncertainty Representation

### Combination Formula

Both signals are combined into a single confidence score using a weighted average:

```
confidence = 0.55 * llm_score + 0.45 * stylo_score
```

**Rationale for weights:** The LLM signal captures semantic and contextual patterns that stylometrics cannot (e.g., "GPT-ish" phrasing). It gets slightly more weight. Stylometrics is a robust cross-check on structural properties the LLM might miss. The 55/45 split keeps stylometrics influential without over-indexing on structural analysis alone.

### Calibrated Thresholds

| Score Range         | Attribution         | Label Variant          |
|---------------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| ≥ 0.75              | `likely_ai`         | High-confidence AI     |
| 0.45 – 0.74         | `uncertain`         | Uncertain              |
| < 0.45              | `likely_human`      | High-confidence human  |

**What 0.5 means:** A score of 0.5 means the two signals are giving mixed or moderate readings — neither strongly AI nor strongly human. The system doesn't know. It will return an "Uncertain" label and give the creator a clear path to appeal. This is intentional: a 0.5 is not a verdict.

**False positive asymmetry:** A false positive (labeling a human's work as AI) is worse than a false negative on a writing platform. The threshold for `likely_ai` is intentionally set high at 0.75 rather than 0.5 to reduce false positives. The "uncertain" band (0.45–0.74) is deliberately wide, catching edge cases where confidence is not strong enough to declare a verdict.

### Calibration Check

During development, test with at least 4 inputs:
1. Clearly AI-generated text → should produce confidence ≥ 0.75
2. Clearly human-written text (casual, messy, personal) → should produce confidence < 0.45
3. Formal human writing (academic) → should produce confidence in the 0.45–0.65 range
4. Lightly edited AI output → should produce confidence in the 0.55–0.75 range

If tests 1 and 2 don't land in their target ranges, adjust the signal normalization, not the thresholds.

## Transparency Label Variants

All three variants are designed for a non-technical reader. The label makes confidence meaningful.

### Variant 1: High-Confidence AI (confidence ≥ 0.75)

```
⚠️ AI-Generated Content Detected

Our analysis suggests this content was likely generated by an AI tool, not written by a human author.

Confidence: High ([SCORE]%)

This label is shown to help readers understand the origin of this content. If you are the creator and believe this is incorrect, you may submit an appeal below.
```

### Variant 2: Uncertain (0.45 ≤ confidence < 0.75)

```
🔍 Origin Uncertain

Our system was unable to determine with confidence whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI.

Confidence in AI origin: Moderate ([SCORE]%)

We don't have enough signal to make a definitive determination. Readers should consider this context. Creators who believe this result is inaccurate may submit an appeal.
```

### Variant 3: High-Confidence Human (confidence < 0.45)

```
✅ Likely Human-Written

Our analysis suggests this content was written by a human author.

Confidence in human origin: High ([SCORE]%)

This content passed our AI-origin detection checks. This label does not constitute a guarantee of authenticity.
```

## Appeals Workflow

### Who can appeal?
Any creator who submitted the original content — identified by `creator_id` in the original submission.

### What information is required?
- `content_id` (required): The unique ID returned by the `/submit` endpoint
- `creator_reasoning` (required): The creator's explanation of why the classification is wrong (free text, min 10 characters)

### What happens when an appeal is submitted?
1. The system looks up the existing log entry for the `content_id`
2. The status is updated from `"classified"` to `"under_review"`
3. The `appeal_reasoning` and `appeal_timestamp` fields are added to the log entry
4. The response confirms the appeal was received and is under review

### What would a human reviewer see?
When a reviewer queries `GET /log`, they see entries with `"status": "under_review"` alongside the original classification data (confidence, signal scores, attribution) and the creator's full reasoning text. This gives them everything needed to make a judgment call.

### Automated re-classification?
Not implemented. The system flags for human review. This is intentional — automated re-classification based on a creator's appeal creates a bypass vulnerability.

## Anticipated Edge Cases

1. **Short poems with simple vocabulary:** A haiku or a short lyric poem with deliberate repetition and minimal punctuation will look structurally similar to AI output under the stylometric signal. The TTR will be low (few unique words), SLV will be low (short, similar lines), and the LLM may also flag the polished minimalism. This is a known false-positive risk. The label design partially mitigates this by requiring high confidence (≥ 0.75) before declaring AI origin.

2. **Non-native English speakers writing formally:** Writers who are non-native English speakers often write in more grammatically structured ways that resemble AI output — careful syntax, minimal slang, lower contraction rates. The LLM signal in particular may over-flag this population because its training data correlates formal grammar with AI generation. This is a fairness concern noted in the README.

3. **Intentionally AI-mimicking human text:** A human writer who deliberately styles their prose after AI outputs (e.g., for parody) will score as AI-generated. The system has no mechanism to detect ironic intent.

4. **Very long texts (>3000 words):** The LLM call truncates input at a practical limit. For very long texts, only a sample is sent, which may not be representative. Mitigation: document this limit and sample from the beginning, middle, and end.

5. **Texts in non-English languages:** Stylometric tokenization is English-centric. Punctuation density baselines differ across languages. The LLM signal may still work cross-lingually but with reduced reliability. Current implementation targets English only.

## Rate Limiting

**Chosen limits:** `10 per minute; 100 per day` per IP address on `POST /submit`

**Reasoning:**
- A real writer submitting their own work might submit a handful of pieces per session. 10 per minute is generous for legitimate use.
- 100 per day prevents a single IP from bulk-testing the system's responses (adversarial probing) while still allowing a prolific creator to submit a full portfolio in a day.
- The appeal endpoint is not rate-limited at the same level — appeals are lower-volume and the friction of requiring a `content_id` already constrains them naturally.
